 The 14th of February also marks another important occasion, much less talked about yet deeply significant: it is Congenital Heart Diseases (CHD) Awareness Day. I personally hold this topic very close to my heart (pun intended!) and I’d like to use this space to spend a couple of words about it.
 CHDs are anatomical anomalies of the heart present from birth and are due to an incomplete or incorrect cardiac development during the first weeks of embryonic life; they may involve the wall of the heart, its valves, the blood vessels, or the position of the arteries and veins. Estimated to affect nearly 1% of newborns, they represent the most common type of birth defect and, unfortunately, the first cause of child mortality. CHDs vary greatly in type and entity and consequently impact people's lives differently: while some lead a “normal” life, approximately one in four of the affected children has a critical heart disease that will require a first emergency surgery within the first month of life and many more throughout it.
 Until a few decades ago, congenital heart defects were fatal within the first years of childhood. Today, thanks to improvements in diagnosis, cutting-edge surgical interventions, and continuous medical research, even patients with very complex clinical pictures have a better prognosis and quality of life.
 Despite these achievements, CHDs remain a major public health concern: there is still no definitive cure for many forms of congenital heart disease, and lifelong medical care is often necessary; furthermore, CHDs are not always clinically evident, making it difficult for affected individuals to get the support they need. Therefore, promoting awareness and supporting research and medical innovation is crucial to ensure better outcomes, better treatments, and a deeper understanding of the battles that CHDs warriors go through everyday.
 Multiple research institutions all over Italy, including our University, are contributing to the advancement of CHD treatments: from biomedical engineering to cutting-edge genetic studies, researchers are actively working on improving both diagnostic tools and therapeutic approaches. Ongoing projects are exploring new factors that might be responsible for the development of CHDs, new surgical techniques, the use of biotechnologies for regenerative medicine, the potential role of genetics in the early detection of CHDs, and so much more.

 Non-fiction Book Recommendation - from Sofia:
&emp;‘She has her mother’s laugh - the powers, perversions, and potential of heredity’, by Carl Zimmer

 Why do children look like their parents? Mendelian genetics offers a clear answer. But what about the ways they don’t? Why can siblings be so different from one another? And does heredity stop at DNA, or is there more to the story? If you’ve ever found yourself wondering about these questions, She Has Her Mother’s Laugh by Carl Zimmer is the perfect book for you.

 Zimmer takes the familiar concept of inheritance and stretches it far beyond the boundaries of DNA, exploring how our understanding of heredity has evolved from ancient misconceptions to cutting-edge genomic research. Rather than treating genetics as a simple set of rules, he reveals a much richer, more complex picture: one where mutations, epigenetics, and even the microbes we carry play a role in shaping who we are. His storytelling blends history, science, and thought-provoking ethical questions, making for an engaging and deeply insightful read.

 As a genomics student, I was particularly fascinated by his discussion of genetic ‘chimeras’: the idea is that an individual isn’t necessarily made up of identical cells with the same DNA. Mutations and variations can arise during development, meaning that different parts of our body might have slightly different genetic codes. This concept challenges the traditional view of a singular, fixed genome and highlights just how dynamic biology truly is. Zimmer’s ability to unpack such complex ideas in an accessible and compelling way is what makes this book a must-read.

 For students and science enthusiasts alike, She Has Her Mother’s Laugh is a reminder that genetics is far more than just Punnett squares and inheritance charts. It’s a story that intersects with medicine, history, and ethics; one that shapes not just our biology, but our identities and societies. Whether you’re passionate about DNA, curious about the future of genetic research, or simply enjoy a well-told science story, this book is an enriching and thought-provoking read.
